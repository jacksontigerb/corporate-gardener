What I do

Three kinds of work.

Consulting, facilitation and coaching. Most organisations end up with a blend, shaped around what they need rather than picked off a shelf. Whatever the mix, I'm always clear about which one I'm doing, and for whom.

Consulting

Working out the leadership and culture your organisation needs next, then making it real.

This is for the people running an organisation that's changing: chief executives, directors, and anyone responsible for how the place is led. They usually arrive with questions like these.

  • “What kind of leadership do we actually need for where we're heading?”
  • “Our values are on the wall. Why don't they show up in how people behave?”
  • “Head office and the front line pull in different directions. How do we get them working as one operation?”

What I actually do

  • I watch the real work. I spend time where the organisation actually runs: in meetings, on site, on board. What people do tells you more than what they say in a survey.
  • I compare three versions of the story. What the organisation says about itself, what its leaders privately believe, and what actually happens in the room. The gaps between them are usually where the problem lives.
  • I report back in plain words. Things like “here's what your best leaders consistently do that the others don't”, not a deck of charts.
  • We design the next steps together. Practical changes to how people lead, meet and give feedback, with agreement upfront on what better will look like.

A recent example

A cruise line wanted its ships and its head office pulling in the same direction. We began with a workshop in Genoa that put senior leaders from ship and shore in one room, working out how the company's value of Better Together should change everyday decisions on both sides.

That grew into longer work on board: watching how ships' leadership teams actually run their operations, coaching them, and feeding what we learned back into the company's plans.

Facilitation

Practical sessions where people work on their real situations, and practise the conversations they've been putting off.

I work with leadership groups, departments and teams. Sometimes a whole tier of leaders, sometimes eight people around one table. It tends to help when:

  • A promise like “speak up” or “better together” needs to become more than a poster.
  • A team wants to reset how it meets, decides and disagrees.
  • New leaders need a shared way of talking about leading people.

How I run a session

  • It starts from your reality. Sessions are built on the pressures of your world and what participants are dealing with that week, not hypothetical case studies.
  • People practise, not just discuss. The conversations they actually need to have, prepared in the room with the colleagues who know the situation.
  • Everyone leaves with something to do. One or two specific actions for the week ahead, not a folder of slides.
  • The plan bends. If something more useful surfaces in the room, we follow it. The point is what changes afterwards, not covering the agenda.

An example

The Quality team at Jaguar Land Rover worked with me over twelve months, using a profiling tool called Insights Discovery to understand how each person works and what they need from each other. The profiles were never the point. The point was how the team met, gave feedback and disagreed long after the sessions ended.

Coaching

For individual leaders and for leadership teams: seeing yourself and your situation clearly enough to change what you do.

Coaching individuals

For leaders stepping into a bigger role, heads of department, and people whose next job looks nothing like their last one. Recent work includes newly appointed C suite leaders across sales, technology, HR, finance and operations, and a Chief Superintendent in the Metropolitan Police.

  • Regular one to one conversations, working on whatever is actually keeping you up at night.
  • Watching you at work where possible. A real meeting rather than a role play, followed by specific feedback on how you come across.
  • Honest attention to patterns. What keeps happening when you're under pressure, and what you want to do about it.
  • Clear ground rules. Goals are agreed with you and, where your employer is paying, openly with them too. You always know who hears what.

Coaching teams

For leadership teams that need to be more honest and more effective with each other, from a hotel department to an executive team.

  • I start by listening and watching. A conversation with each person, then time watching the team actually work.
  • We work on the real stuff in the room. How you meet, decide, hand over and challenge each other. Not trust exercises.
  • It ends in experiments. A redesigned team meeting, a different way of doing handovers, and a check later on whether it stuck.

An example

A restaurant leadership team on a cruise ship needed a reset mid season. I joined the ship, watched service and the daily meetings, then worked with the leaders on how they ran the operation, and each other. The follow up happened where it counts: in the next day's service, not in a workshop.
